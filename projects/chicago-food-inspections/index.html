Chicago Bakeries Didn't Get Dirtier in 2019.
The Rulebook Just Got Stricter.

By Gayatri Mahindrakar & Harisankar Kartha · IS 445, Group 5

Between 2017 and 2019, the share of Chicago bakery inspections ending in a clean pass plummeted from roughly 70% to under 19%. Restaurants and grocery stores fell almost as far. The cause wasn't a citywide hygiene collapse — it was a single regulatory change that almost no one outside of the Chicago Department of Public Health noticed at the time.

Every year, the Chicago Department of Public Health (CDPH) sends inspectors to tens of thousands of food establishments — restaurants, grocery stores, schools, bakeries, daycares, long-term-care facilities. Each visit ends in one of three outcomes: Pass, Pass with Conditions, or Fail. Across the 246,990 inspections we analyzed from 2010 through 2025, restaurants alone account for roughly 175,000 of them — nearly six times more than any other facility type.

For most of the 2010s, somewhere around two-thirds of those inspections came back as straight passes. Then 2018 happened.

The July 2018 Cliff

In July 2018, CDPH overhauled how its inspectors classify violations. Conditions that had previously been logged as minor were reclassified as "priority" or "priority foundation" issues — the kind that, under the new framework, could push an inspection from Pass into Pass with Conditions or even Fail. Nothing about the underlying cleanliness of Chicago kitchens changed overnight. The yardstick did.

Different Sectors, Different Recoveries

What's most interesting in the post-2019 data isn't the drop itself — it's who recovered, and how fast. Childcare facilities (Children's Services Facilities and the two daycare categories) climbed back to pass rates of 65–77% by 2025. Restaurants, grocery stores, and bakeries did not. They sit stubbornly in the 60–65% range, even five years on.

A second, smaller dip in 2020 cuts across every facility type. That one's easier to explain: COVID-19 collapsed the volume of in-person inspections, and the inspections that did happen tended to focus on the most at-risk establishments. Both effects show up clearly in the heatmap below.

What This Means for Chicago Diners

A reader scanning a 2024 inspection report and a 2014 report side-by-side could be forgiven for thinking Chicago's food scene got measurably worse. It didn't — at least not by the underlying measure. What changed is the definition of "good enough." That distinction matters: pass-rate trends are increasingly used by journalists, ratings sites, and even insurance underwriters as a proxy for food safety. When the definition shifts under everyone's feet without a corresponding public communication campaign, the data tells a story the city didn't intend to tell.

The good news: even under the stricter post-2018 framework, most Chicago establishments still pass on their first try. The not-as-good news: the gap between childcare facilities (which face mandatory annual inspections and high-stakes enforcement) and the rest of the food economy is real and persistent. Where you eat, increasingly, predicts how closely the city is paying attention.
