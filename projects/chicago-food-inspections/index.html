Food Safety · Chicago · 2010–2025

Chicago Bakeries Didn't Get Dirtier in 2019.
The Rulebook Just Got Stricter.

By Gayatri Mahindrakar & Harisankar Kartha · IS 445, Group 5

Between 2017 and 2019, the share of Chicago bakery inspections ending in a clean pass plummeted from roughly 70% to under 19%. Restaurants and grocery stores fell almost as far. The cause wasn't a citywide hygiene collapse — it was a single regulatory change that almost no one outside of the Chicago Department of Public Health noticed at the time.

Every year, the Chicago Department of Public Health (CDPH) sends inspectors to tens of thousands of food establishments — restaurants, grocery stores, schools, bakeries, daycares, long-term-care facilities. Each visit ends in one of three outcomes: Pass, Pass with Conditions, or Fail. Across the 246,990 inspections we analyzed from 2010 through 2025, restaurants alone account for roughly 175,000 of them — nearly six times more than any other facility type.

For most of the 2010s, somewhere around two-thirds of those inspections came back as straight passes. Then 2018 happened.

The July 2018 Cliff

In July 2018, CDPH overhauled how its inspectors classify violations. Conditions that had previously been logged as minor were reclassified as "priority" or "priority foundation" issues — the kind that, under the new framework, could push an inspection from Pass into Pass with Conditions or even Fail. Nothing about the underlying cleanliness of Chicago kitchens changed overnight. The yardstick did.

The result, visible in nearly every panel above, is a synchronized collapse of pass rates between 2017 and 2019. The chart below makes the size of the drop concrete.

Different Sectors, Different Recoveries

By 2025 most facility types had clawed back to roughly where they sat before the rule change — restaurants and bakeries hovering in the low 60s, childcare facilities in the mid-70s, schools and long-term-care somewhere in between. What's striking is that the pre-2018 hierarchy reappeared almost intact: the sectors that ran high before the cliff (Children's Services Facilities, the two daycare categories) are running high again, and the sectors that ran lower (Restaurants, Grocery Stores, Long Term Care) are still lower. The cliff was universal, but the recovery wasn't a level-up — it was a return to baseline.

A second, smaller dip in 2020 cuts across every facility type. That one's easier to explain: COVID-19 collapsed the volume of in-person inspections, and the inspections that did happen tended to focus on the most at-risk establishments. Both effects show up clearly in the heatmap below.

The COVID Inspection Gap

The pass-rate story is really a story about which inspections happen, not just how they're scored. To put inspection volume in context, we joined the inspection records to a second City of Chicago dataset: Business Licenses, which records every food-related license the city issues. Linking the two by license number lets us count, for each year, what share of Chicago's licensed food establishments actually got inspected — a rough proxy for how well CDPH's inspection system was keeping up with its own caseload.

For most of the 2010s, that share sat near 100%. Then COVID hit. In 2020 and 2021, coverage collapsed to roughly 78%. In real numbers: nearly 2,900 licensed food establishments operated for an entire year without a single inspection. The system didn't fully recover until 2024 — four years later. A smaller dip is also visible in 2018, the year CDPH overhauled its violation framework, suggesting that inspections under the new rules took longer per visit and fewer establishments got reached.

What This Means for Chicago Diners

A reader scanning a 2024 inspection report and a 2014 report side-by-side could be forgiven for thinking Chicago's food scene got measurably worse. It didn't — at least not by the underlying measure. What changed is the definition of "good enough." That distinction matters: pass-rate trends are increasingly used by journalists, ratings sites, and even insurance underwriters as a proxy for food safety. When the definition shifts under everyone's feet without a corresponding public communication campaign, the data tells a story the city didn't intend to tell.

The good news: even under the stricter post-2018 framework, most Chicago establishments still pass on their first try. The not-as-good news: the gap between childcare facilities (which face mandatory annual inspections and high-stakes enforcement) and the rest of the food economy is real and persistent. Where you eat, increasingly, predicts how closely the city is paying attention.

Methodology & Sources

Primary dataset

Chicago Food Inspections. City of Chicago Open Data Portal, maintained by the Chicago Department of Public Health. data.cityofchicago.org/Health-Human-Services/Food-Inspections/4ijn-s7e5. Accessed 2026. 309,328 raw records spanning January 2010 through April 2026; filtered to 246,990 records with valid Pass/Fail/Pass-w-Conditions outcomes, valid risk levels, and complete years (2010–2025), restricted to the 8 most-inspected facility types.

Contextual dataset (used in coverage-gap chart)

Chicago Business Licenses. City of Chicago Open Data Portal. data.cityofchicago.org/Community-Economic-Development/Business-Licenses/r5kz-chrr. Filtered server-side via the Socrata API to 14 food-related license categories (Retail Food Establishment, Wholesale Food, Mobile Food, Pop-Up Food, Shared Kitchen, Food Peddler, and Navy Pier Food Vendor variants). For each year 2010–2025, we counted unique licenses active on July 1 and joined to unique inspected establishments by license number to compute annual inspection coverage.

Regulatory background

Chicago Department of Public Health, Food Protection Division. Public announcement of revised violation classification framework, July 2018.
